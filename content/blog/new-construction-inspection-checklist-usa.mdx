Buying a new construction home in the United States is exciting — but the closing process can be rushed, and many buyers are not prepared for the walkthrough that determines what gets fixed before they move in. Most builders provide two inspection opportunities: a **blue tape walkthrough** (where defects are marked with blue painter's tape) and a **final walkthrough** shortly before closing. This guide helps you make the most of both.

## New Construction vs. Existing Home Inspections

One of the most common misconceptions is that new construction homes don't need inspections because "everything is brand new." In reality, construction defects in new homes are remarkably common — and far cheaper to fix before closing than after.

Unlike an existing home inspection (which assesses age-related wear), a new construction inspection focuses on:
- Workmanship quality
- Code compliance
- Completeness of construction
- Proper installation of all systems
- Defects caused during construction (damage, incorrect installation, missing items)

You have the right to hire a third-party inspector for new construction in all US states. Don't waive this right. A professional inspector will identify defects your builder's team might miss — or choose not to mention.

## The Builder's Warranty: What US Buyers Are Entitled To

Unlike Australia and Ireland, the US does not have a single national new home warranty scheme. Warranties are provided directly by the builder and vary widely. Common structures:

- **1 year:** Workmanship and materials (most defects)
- **2 years:** Mechanical systems (HVAC, plumbing, electrical)
- **10 years:** Structural defects

Some builders also offer third-party warranties through providers like 2-10 Home Buyers Warranty or RWC (Residential Warranty Company). These provide additional protection if the builder goes out of business or disputes a claim.

**Always read your warranty documents before closing.** Know exactly what's covered, for how long, and what the reporting requirements are.

## The Blue Tape Walkthrough

Most national and regional builders schedule a walkthrough 1–4 weeks before closing where you walk through the home and mark defects with blue painter's tape. A builder representative accompanies you and notes each item.

**Tips for your blue tape walkthrough:**

1. **Do it in daylight hours.** Schedule the walkthrough for mid-morning when natural light is fullest.
2. **Don't rush.** You're entitled to take as long as you need. If the builder representative is trying to move too quickly, politely slow down.
3. **Test everything, not just look.** Plug in a socket tester, turn on every light, run every faucet.
4. **Photograph everything.** Even if your builder is recording it too, your own timestamped photos are your evidence.
5. **Get a copy.** Before you leave, photograph the builder representative's completed list. Better yet, ask for an email copy to be sent to you within 24 hours.

## Room-by-Room Inspection Checklist

### Exterior

1. **Grading and drainage:** The ground around the foundation should slope away from the house — minimum 6 inches of drop in the first 10 feet. Improper grading causes basement flooding and foundation damage.
2. **Foundation:** Inspect visible foundation walls for cracks. Hairline cracks in poured concrete are common; horizontal cracks are serious.
3. **Roof:** Check from the ground that shingles are laid in straight lines, hip and ridge caps are neatly finished, and no shingles are cracked or lifted.
4. **Gutters and downspouts:** Should be correctly pitched and discharge at least 6 feet from the foundation via extensions or splash blocks.
5. **Driveway and walkways:** Check for cracks, especially at expansion joints.
6. **Windows and exterior doors:** Open, close, and lock every one. Check weatherstripping is complete.
7. **Garage door:** Test the auto-reverse. Place a 2x4 flat on the ground in the door's path — the door must reverse when it contacts it. This is a code requirement.

### Main Floor Living Areas

1. Test every outlet with a socket tester — check polarity and correct wiring.
2. Test every light switch and ceiling fan (all speeds, check for wobble).
3. Inspect walls and ceilings in natural light for paint finish quality, drywall nail pops, and cracks at corners.
4. Check all interior door swings — they should clear the floor and all trim by at least ½ inch, and latch on first close.
5. Inspect flooring for lippage, damage, and gaps at transitions.
6. Check fireplace if present — damper should open and close fully.

### Kitchen

1. Open every cabinet door and drawer. All should operate smoothly and align correctly.
2. Check countertop seams — they should be flush with no visible step or gap.
3. Run the kitchen faucet — check hot water arrives, inspect under the sink for leaks.
4. Test the garbage disposal. Run water while operating.
5. Test the dishwasher — run a short cycle and check under the cabinet and at the door gasket for leaks.
6. Test the range, oven, and hood exhaust on all settings.
7. Check the refrigerator ice maker line connection if applicable.

### Bathrooms

1. Check all tiling for lippage, cracked tiles, and consistent grout.
2. Flush every toilet — the flush should be vigorous and the cistern refill within 60 seconds.
3. Run all showers — check pressure, hot water delivery, and drainage speed.
4. Test GFCI outlets — press test, outlet should lose power; press reset, outlet should restore. Required within 6 feet of water sources.
5. Check exhaust fans — they should operate and not rattle.
6. Verify caulking at tub/shower surrounds is complete.

### Bedrooms

1. Test every outlet.
2. Check closet lighting and shelving installation.
3. Verify windows operate correctly — all locks engage.
4. Check window screens are fitted and undamaged.

### Attic

1. Check insulation depth — minimum R-values vary by climate zone. Ask your builder what R-value was installed.
2. No signs of moisture or daylight visible through the roof deck.
3. Verify ventilation baffles are in place at eaves to prevent insulation from blocking airflow.
4. Check that bathroom fans duct to the exterior — not just into the attic (a very common code violation).

### Basement or Crawlspace

1. Check walls for efflorescence (white salt staining — indicates moisture intrusion).
2. Inspect the sump pump if present — pour a bucket of water into the pit to verify the pump activates.
3. Check vapor barrier in crawlspace is correctly installed and undamaged.
4. No moisture, mold, or standing water.

### HVAC System

1. Test heating and cooling — both should respond to the thermostat and deliver conditioned air from every vent.
2. Check all vents — no blockages, dampers open.
3. Verify the air filter is accessible and correctly installed.
4. Check condensate drain line is clear and discharged to an appropriate location.

### Electrical Panel

1. All breakers should be labelled.
2. Panel should not have any double-tapped breakers (two wires on a single breaker — a code violation unless the breaker is rated for it).
3. Main shut-off should operate freely.

## 5 Items US New Build Buyers Most Often Miss

1. **Bathroom exhaust fans venting into the attic.** This is a code violation that causes moisture damage to the attic structure. Check by going into the attic during a walkthrough.
2. **Garage-to-house door.** This must be a 20-minute fire-rated door with a self-closing mechanism. Check the door label and test the self-closer.
3. **GFCI outlets near water.** Kitchen countertop outlets, all bathroom outlets, and garage outlets must be GFCI protected. Test every one.
4. **Sump pump operation.** If the home has a sump pump, most buyers never test it. Pour a bucket of water in the pit — if the pump doesn't activate, that's a warranty item.
5. **Proper grading slope.** Get low to the ground and look along the foundation. The ground must slope away — builders frequently fail this one.
