The builder's warranty is one of the most important documents you'll receive when closing on a new construction home — and one of the least read. Many homeowners only reach for it when something goes wrong, at which point they discover coverage they assumed they had doesn't exist, or that they've missed a reporting deadline.

This guide explains US new construction builder warranties clearly, covering what's typically included, what's excluded, how to file a claim, and how third-party warranty programs work.

## How US Builder Warranties Work

Unlike Ireland, the UK, and Australia, the United States does not have a single mandatory national warranty scheme for new homes. Builder warranties are provided directly by the builder and regulated at the state level. This means coverage varies significantly between builders and states.

However, most warranties follow a common structure:

### 1-Year Warranty: Workmanship and Materials

The most comprehensive coverage period. During year 1, the builder is typically responsible for:
- All visible defects in workmanship — paint, drywall, flooring, tiling, cabinetry
- Plumbing, electrical, and HVAC defects related to installation
- Grading and drainage issues
- Door and window operation
- Any item that was incorrect, damaged, or incomplete at delivery

### 2-Year Warranty: Systems

Many builders extend coverage specifically for major systems:
- HVAC (heating, ventilation, and air conditioning)
- Electrical systems
- Plumbing systems
- Water infiltration through the exterior envelope (roof, windows, doors)

### 10-Year Warranty: Structural Defects

Structural coverage is the longest but the most narrowly defined:
- Foundation defects
- Load-bearing wall failures
- Roof structure failure
- Floor system structural defects
- Any defect that compromises the structural integrity of the home

**Note:** "Structural" is defined narrowly. Cosmetic cracks in drywall, even if caused by settlement, are generally not structural.

## Third-Party Warranty Programs

Many builders supplement their own warranty with a third-party insurance-backed warranty. The most common providers are:

- **2-10 Home Buyers Warranty (2-10 HBW)** — widely used; provides 1-2-10 structure coverage with defined terms
- **RWC (Residential Warranty Company)** — similar 1-2-10 structure; insurance-backed
- **Maverick American Warranty** — smaller regional provider

Third-party warranties are valuable because they provide protection if your builder goes out of business, and they have defined dispute resolution processes that are easier to navigate than suing a builder.

Always verify whether your builder's warranty is self-insured (only as good as the builder's continued business) or backed by a third-party insurance provider.

## What Builder Warranties Do NOT Cover

This is where most disputes arise. Standard exclusions include:

**Owner-Caused Damage:**
- Any damage caused by you, your tenants, or your contractors
- Damage from failure to perform required maintenance (filter changes, gutter cleaning)
- Modifications made without builder approval

**Normal Wear and Tear:**
- Paint fading and scuffing
- Carpet wear
- Caulk shrinkage (normal; requires periodic re-application)
- Minor drywall nail pops (especially after year 1)
- Wood flooring expansion/contraction (within code tolerances)

**Weather and Acts of God:**
- Damage from storms, floods, and lightning
- Freeze damage from failure to maintain adequate heat in winter

**Non-Covered Components:**
- Appliances (covered by manufacturer warranties)
- Landscaping, unless agreed in the contract
- Pools and spas
- Detached garages or outbuildings (check your specific contract)

**Defects Not Reported in Writing:**
This is the critical one. Most builder warranties require written notification of defects. Some have specific deadlines — if you notice a defect in month 11 and your 1-year warranty expires in month 12, you must report in writing before the deadline.

## How to File a Builder Warranty Claim

1. **Document the defect.** Photograph it clearly — wide shot for context, close-up for detail. Note the date you first noticed it.

2. **Submit in writing.** Most builders have a warranty portal or email address for warranty requests. Never rely on a verbal conversation. Include:
   - Your name, address, and closing date
   - Clear description of the defect
   - Location within the home
   - Date first noticed
   - Photographs attached

3. **Track the response.** Most builder warranties specify a response timeframe — typically 10–30 days for non-emergency items. Follow up in writing if you don't receive a response.

4. **Escalate if needed.** If the builder refuses a valid warranty item:
   - Review your warranty document for the dispute resolution clause
   - Third-party warranty providers have formal escalation processes
   - Your state attorney general's consumer protection office handles warranty disputes
   - NHIC (National Home Improvement Council) and local builder associations can sometimes mediate

## State-Specific Warranty Requirements

Several US states have enacted laws that supplement or override builder warranty terms:

- **California:** California Right to Repair Act (SB 800) provides specific defect standards and a mandatory dispute resolution process before litigation
- **Florida:** Florida Homeowners' Construction Recovery Fund provides additional protection
- **Texas:** Texas has specific residential construction liability laws and a mandatory notice process
- **New Jersey:** New Home Warranty and Builders' Registration Act provides specific warranties

If you're in one of these states, your rights may be broader than your builder's warranty document suggests. Research your state's specific laws.

## 5 Steps to Protect Your Builder Warranty Rights

1. **Read your warranty documents at closing** — not a year later. Know exactly what's covered, for how long, and how to file a claim.
2. **Conduct a thorough inspection at the blue tape walkthrough** and again on your own before closing. SnapSnag guides you through 86 checks.
3. **Set calendar reminders** at month 11 and month 23 for follow-up inspections. Submit any new items in writing before the relevant deadline.
4. **Report everything in writing, even minor items.** A small leak reported at month 10 and fixed at month 13 (after the 1-year deadline) may not be covered if it recurs.
5. **Keep all correspondence, inspection reports, and photographs permanently.** Warranty disputes can arise years later.
