New construction homes in the United States come with one significant advantage over older homes: everything should be brand new. Yet research consistently shows that defects in new builds are not only common — they're expected. A 2023 survey by the National Association of Home Builders (NAHB) found that the majority of new home buyers reported at least one warranty service call in their first year of ownership.

Understanding the most common defects puts you in a much stronger position during your walkthrough and in the months after closing. Here's what to look for, why these problems occur, and what to do when you find them.

## Why New Construction Homes Have Defects

It might seem counterintuitive that brand-new homes have defects, but several systemic factors make them almost inevitable:

**Speed of construction.** Production builders operate on tight schedules. A typical tract home is framed, dried in, and finished in 90–120 days. Wet materials — concrete, drywall compound, caulk — are often covered before they've fully cured.

**Subcontracted labor.** Every trade is subcontracted, often to the lowest bidder. Plumbers, electricians, framers, and finish carpenters may never meet — and quality control falls through the gaps.

**Weather exposure.** Framing lumber, OSB sheathing, and other materials are routinely exposed to rain during construction. Moisture absorbed before the building is dried in can cause long-term issues.

**Rapid workforce turnover.** The construction labor market has high turnover, meaning the person hanging your drywall may have weeks of experience rather than years.

None of this is unusual — it's the reality of production homebuilding. Your job is to document every defect you find so your builder's warranty obligation is clear.

## The 10 Most Common New Construction Defects

### 1. Drywall Nail Pops and Cracks

The single most frequently reported new construction defect in the US. As lumber framing dries out after construction (losing moisture absorbed during building), it shrinks slightly, pushing drywall screws and nails outward — creating small bumps or circles on wall and ceiling surfaces.

Nail pops are normal and covered under the 1-year workmanship warranty. Your builder should fill and repaint them during the warranty period.

Cracks at wall/ceiling junctions and around door and window openings are also normal settlement — they should be reported and repaired under year-1 coverage.

**What to look for:** Stand in the center of each room and look at walls and ceilings at a raking angle in natural light. Shadows reveal pops and cracks that are invisible when viewed straight on.

### 2. Grading and Drainage Problems

Improper lot grading is one of the most consequential defects — and one that many buyers miss at closing because it only becomes apparent during rain.

Building codes require the ground to slope away from the foundation — minimum 6 inches of drop within the first 10 feet. When grading is inadequate, rainwater pools against the foundation and eventually infiltrates the basement or crawlspace, causing moisture damage, mold, and in severe cases, foundation cracking.

**What to look for:** Walk the perimeter after rainfall. Look for water pooling within 3 feet of the foundation. Check that downspout extensions direct water well away from the house.

### 3. HVAC Issues

Heating and cooling systems are the most expensive mechanical systems in a new home and among the most commonly defective:

- **Undersized or improperly balanced ductwork** — some rooms run hotter or colder than others
- **Refrigerant leaks** — often not apparent until the system runs under load in summer
- **Condensate drain line clogs** — can cause water damage to ceilings below air handlers
- **Bathroom fans vented into the attic** — an extremely common code violation; fans must duct to the exterior
- **Missing or inadequate attic insulation** — affects system efficiency and comfort

**What to look for:** Test heating and cooling on your walkthrough. Stand at every supply vent and feel for airflow. In the attic, look for bath fan ducts terminating in the attic space rather than through the roof or eaves.

### 4. Plumbing Defects

- **Slow drains** — construction debris lodged in drain lines is common
- **Inadequate water pressure** — often caused by debris in supply lines or incorrectly sized pipe
- **Dripping faucets** — factory-set hot water temperatures too high for valve seats
- **Leaks under kitchen sinks** — drain connections not properly tightened during installation
- **Water hammer** — banging pipes caused by missing or incorrectly installed air chambers

**What to look for:** Run every faucet, shower, and hose bib. Run the kitchen faucet and immediately inspect under the sink. Flush every toilet simultaneously to check for pressure drops.

### 5. Electrical Problems

- **Reversed polarity outlets** — hot and neutral wires connected backwards. Not always immediately apparent but can damage electronics. A simple socket tester reveals this instantly.
- **GFCI outlets not installed** — required by code within 6 feet of water sources in kitchens, bathrooms, garages, and outdoors
- **Switches wired to wrong fixtures** — particularly common in rooms with multiple switches and fixtures
- **Missing arc-fault circuit interrupter (AFCI) breakers** — required by code in most living areas since the 2014 NEC

**What to look for:** Bring a $10 outlet tester and test every outlet. Press the test button on every GFCI outlet. Test every light switch and confirm it operates the correct fixture.

### 6. Window and Door Problems

- **Windows that stick or won't latch** — caused by framing movement, improper shimming, or oversized rough openings
- **Exterior doors with gaps** — visible light around the frame indicates poor installation or missing weatherstripping
- **Condensation between panes** — indicates a failed insulating glass seal
- **Missing window screens** — surprisingly common; verify every window has a screen
- **Sliding door track misalignment** — patio doors frequently run out of alignment

### 7. Exterior and Roofing Defects

- **Missing or inadequate caulking** at all penetrations (pipes, wires, window flanges, door frames) — these are the primary entry points for water
- **Improperly flashed valleys and penetrations** — flashings at chimneys, skylights, and roof penetrations are common leak points
- **Improper deck ledger flashing** — a structural and water intrusion concern if present
- **Brick veneer weep holes blocked** — weep holes allow moisture to escape from behind brick; if blocked by mortar, moisture has nowhere to go

### 8. Insulation Deficiencies

- **Missing or incomplete insulation** in exterior walls — sometimes caused by wiring or plumbing trades cutting through insulation batts
- **Insufficient attic insulation depth** — minimum R-values vary by climate zone
- **Unsealed penetrations** — every hole through the top plate of interior walls into the attic is a major air leakage point if not sealed

### 9. Concrete and Masonry Cracks

- **Garage slab cracks** — small hairline cracks are normal; cracks wider than ¼ inch are defects
- **Basement floor cracks** — similar; some cracking is normal, significant cracking or cracking with moisture is not
- **Stucco cracking** — particularly common in climates with significant temperature swings

### 10. Finish Quality Issues

- **Paint misses and holidays** — areas where paint coverage is incomplete, especially on ceilings
- **Poorly fitted trim** — gaps at mitre joints, nail holes not filled, inconsistent reveals
- **Kitchen cabinet adjustment** — doors and drawers misaligned from installation; requires adjustment of European-style hinges

## How to Get These Fixed Under Warranty

1. **Document before you move anything in.** Furniture covers floor defects and blocks wall cracks from view. Do your full inspection before move-in.
2. **Submit your entire list in one written document within the first month.** Builders find it easier to schedule one response rather than responding to items trickling in.
3. **Reference your warranty document.** If an item is explicitly covered, quote the relevant section.
4. **Set a 30-day response deadline.** If you haven't heard back, send a follow-up in writing citing the original submission date.
5. **Escalate if ignored.** Your state's contractor licensing board and attorney general's consumer protection office take warranty violations seriously.
