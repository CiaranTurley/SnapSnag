If you've bought a new build home in Ireland, your property is almost certainly covered by a HomeBond warranty. But many homeowners have only a vague understanding of what HomeBond actually covers, how long the protection lasts, and — critically — what they need to do to preserve their entitlements.

This guide explains HomeBond clearly, covers the most common gaps in coverage that catch homeowners out, and tells you exactly what to do before your warranty period expires.

## What Is HomeBond?

HomeBond (formerly the National House Building Guarantee Company) is Ireland's leading structural guarantee scheme for new homes. It was established to protect homebuyers from defects in new build properties when builders are unable or unwilling to carry out repairs.

HomeBond membership is a legal requirement under planning conditions for most new residential developments in Ireland. When you buy a new build, your solicitor should confirm that the property is covered by a HomeBond guarantee — and provide you with the HomeBond documentation.

If your property doesn't have HomeBond cover, ask your solicitor about alternative warranties — some builders use Premier Guarantee or Structural Defects Insurance (SDI) instead.

## What HomeBond Covers

HomeBond provides a **10-year structural guarantee** from the date of completion, but the coverage varies by year:

### Years 1 and 2 — Builder's Defects Liability Period

During the first two years, your **builder** is directly responsible for repairing all defects reported in writing. HomeBond acts as a guarantee if the builder fails to carry out the repairs.

Covered in years 1–2:
- Plasterwork defects (cracks, delamination, uneven finish)
- Plumbing faults (leaks, inadequate hot water, poor drainage)
- Electrical faults (incorrectly wired sockets, failed light fittings)
- Joinery defects (sticking doors, warped frames, misaligned windows)
- Tiling defects (hollow tiles, poor grouting, lippage)
- Heating system faults
- Roof covering defects visible from ground level
- External render or brickwork defects
- Kitchen fitting defects

**What this means for you:** Any defect you identify in years 1 and 2 should be reported in writing to your builder first. HomeBond only steps in if the builder doesn't respond or refuses to carry out the repairs.

### Years 1–5 — Water Ingress

Water penetration through the external envelope (roof, walls, windows, doors) is covered for the first five years. This includes:
- Roof leaks
- Damp through external walls
- Water ingress around window frames
- Defective flashings and valleys

To claim under water ingress cover, you must have reported the issue to your builder in years 1–2 and they failed to repair it.

### Years 1–10 — Structural Defects

HomeBond provides structural cover for the full 10-year period. After year 2, HomeBond itself — rather than the builder — will arrange and fund the repair of structural defects.

Structural defects covered:
- Foundation failure or significant settlement
- Load-bearing wall failure
- Roof structure collapse or significant deflection
- Floor structure defects
- Significant cracking that affects structural integrity

**Note:** HomeBond defines structural defects narrowly. Cosmetic cracking (such as hairline plaster cracks that are common in all new builds) is generally not covered after year 2.

## What HomeBond Does NOT Cover

Understanding the exclusions is just as important as knowing what's included:

- **Contents and personal belongings** — HomeBond covers the structure, not what's inside it.
- **Wear and tear** — Normal deterioration over time is not covered.
- **Cosmetic defects after year 2** — Paint fading, minor hairline cracks, and surface blemishes are not covered structurally.
- **Defects not reported in writing during years 1–2** — This is the most common way homeowners lose their entitlements.
- **Damage caused by the homeowner** — Any alterations or repairs carried out without planning permission or building regulations approval.
- **Boundary walls and fencing** — Generally not covered unless they are structural retaining walls.
- **Landscaping and driveways** — Unless specified in your contract, these are typically excluded.
- **Appliances** — White goods and kitchen appliances have their own manufacturer warranties.

## How to Make a HomeBond Claim

### Step 1: Report to Your Builder in Writing

All defects must first be reported in writing to your builder. HomeBond will not process a claim unless you can demonstrate that you've given the builder a reasonable opportunity to repair the defect.

Send your defect report:
- By email (with read receipt or delivery confirmation)
- To both the site manager and the customer care department
- With a clear numbered list of defects and supporting photographs

### Step 2: Allow a Reasonable Period for Repair

Builders typically have 30 days to respond and 60–90 days to carry out repairs. However, if there's a safety concern (such as an electrical fault or roof leak allowing water ingress), you can request urgent action.

### Step 3: Issue a Formal Written Demand

If the builder hasn't responded within 30 days, send a formal written demand setting a further 14-day deadline and stating your intention to contact HomeBond.

### Step 4: Contact HomeBond

If the builder fails to repair within a reasonable timeframe, contact HomeBond at **homebond.ie** or call **01 677 9090**. HomeBond will:
1. Appoint an independent inspector to verify the defect
2. Notify the builder of their liability
3. If the builder still doesn't act, arrange repair and pursue the cost from the builder

## The Timing Trap: Why Snagging Before Year 2 Ends Is Critical

The most common HomeBond-related complaint from Irish homeowners is: *"I reported it verbally, but I didn't put it in writing."* Or: *"I thought I had more time."*

Here's the critical rule: **If you don't report a defect in writing before the end of year 2, you lose your right to have that defect repaired under HomeBond's builder's liability period.** You would then need to rely on the HomeBond scheme directly — which covers structural defects but not the broader range of finish defects covered in years 1–2.

Many Irish new build homeowners find defects in year 3 or 4 that were actually present at completion — paint adhesion failures, movement cracks, plumbing faults that developed slowly — but by then, it's too late for the full builder liability cover.

**The solution:** Do a comprehensive snag list at handover, do a follow-up inspection at 11 months (just before year 1 ends), and do a final check at 23 months (just before year 2 ends). Document everything and report in writing.

## 5 Things to Do to Protect Your HomeBond Entitlements

1. **Get your HomeBond documentation from your solicitor.** Know your exact completion date — your warranty period runs from there.
2. **Do a full snag inspection at handover** and submit your list in writing within 7 days.
3. **Do a follow-up inspection at 11 months.** Look for issues that have developed since you moved in — settling cracks, water staining after winter rain, heating faults that only appear in cold weather.
4. **Do a final inspection at 23 months.** This is your last chance to submit defects under the full builder liability period.
5. **Keep copies of everything** — your snag list, all correspondence, and builder acknowledgements.

## HomeBond vs CIRI: What's the Difference?

HomeBond is a warranty scheme that guarantees the standard of construction. The Construction Industry Register Ireland (CIRI) is a voluntary register of competent contractors. If your builder is CIRI-registered, they have committed to certain standards of practice.

If you have a dispute with your builder, you can also file a complaint with CIRI — though this is a separate process from HomeBond and doesn't provide financial protection.
