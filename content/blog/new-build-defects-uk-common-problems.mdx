Despite tighter building regulations and increasing demand for quality, new build defects remain stubbornly common in the UK. Research consistently shows that the vast majority of new build buyers report problems in the first two years — from minor cosmetic issues to more significant structural concerns.

Understanding the most common defects means you know exactly where to look during your snagging inspection — and you're less likely to be fobbed off with "that's normal" when it isn't.

## Why Do New Build Defects Happen?

Before diving into the defects themselves, it helps to understand why they occur:

- **Speed of construction:** Build programmes are tight. Finishing trades often follow each other within days, and wet materials (plaster, concrete, screed) don't have time to dry fully before the next trade moves in.
- **Subcontracted labour:** Most large housebuilders subcontract every trade. Quality control between subcontractors is inconsistent.
- **Inexperienced supervision:** Site managers often oversee multiple plots simultaneously and can't be everywhere.
- **Weather:** Brickwork laid in frost, plaster applied in cold conditions, and timber installed before it's properly dried all lead to future defects.

None of this is your problem to solve — it's your builder's. But understanding the causes helps you identify where to look.

## The Most Common UK New Build Defects

### 1. Plasterwork Cracks and Poor Finish

The most frequently reported cosmetic defect. Plasterwork cracks fall into two categories:

**Normal settlement cracks:** Hairline cracks at corners, wall/ceiling junctions, and around door frames. These occur as the building dries out and are generally cosmetic. They should still be reported and filled in year 1.

**Defective plasterwork:** Larger cracks (over 3mm), cracks that reappear after filling, plaster that sounds hollow when tapped, or areas where the skim coat is delaminating from the background. These indicate poor application and should be fully reskimmed.

*What to look for:* Stand in the centre of each room and look at walls at a raking angle in natural light. Shadow will reveal undulations and cracks that are invisible when viewed straight on.

### 2. Poorly Fitted Kitchen Units

Kitchen defects are extremely common and often obvious:

- Cabinet doors that won't close fully or don't align with adjacent doors
- Drawers that fall off their runners or don't close flush
- Handles at inconsistent heights or angles
- Worktop joints that are uneven or have visible gaps
- Plinths that are not level or have gaps where they meet the floor

*What to look for:* Open and close every door and drawer at least twice. Check alignment from a low angle — this reveals racking and twist that isn't visible from standing height.

### 3. Tile Defects

Tiling problems are common in both kitchens and bathrooms:

- **Lippage:** Uneven tile edges where adjacent tiles are not flush. Run your finger across the grout line — you shouldn't feel a step.
- **Hollow tiles:** An adhesive bond failure. Tap each tile firmly with a knuckle — a hollow sound indicates the tile may eventually de-bond.
- **Inconsistent grout:** Varying grout line widths, or grout that has been wiped across the tile face leaving a haze.
- **Missing sealant:** Silicon sealant at bath/shower/basin edges must be continuous. Gaps allow water ingress.
- **Cracked tiles:** Visible cracks from impact or thermal movement — particularly on large format tiles.

### 4. UPVC Window and Door Issues

UPVC joinery defects are widespread:

- Windows or doors that stick when opening or closing — often caused by thermal expansion or misalignment
- Draught seals that are compressed in some areas but not others
- Hinges that are misaligned, causing the sash to drop
- Condensation between double-glazed panes — indicates a failed seal in the insulating glass unit
- Missing or incorrectly operating window restrictors (required by building regulations above ground level)

*How to check:* Open every window and door fully, then close and lock them. On a breezy day, hold your hand near frame edges to detect draughts.

### 5. Drainage Issues

Both internal and external drainage defects are common:

- **Slow shower drainage:** Drains blocked with construction debris or incorrectly trapped
- **Gurgling pipes:** Inadequate trap depths or poorly designed waste systems causing siphonage
- **External drainage:** Paths and driveways that slope towards the house rather than away — this causes damp ingress over time
- **Blocked gutters and downpipes:** Construction debris left in guttering is a very common problem

### 6. Electrical Faults

Electrical defects are less visible but detectable:

- Sockets that are not wired correctly — a £5 socket tester will reveal polarity faults instantly
- Light switches wired to the wrong fitting
- Sockets or switches that are not level or flush with the wall surface
- Consumer unit labels that don't match the circuits they describe
- Missing or incorrectly positioned sockets compared to the specification

### 7. Heating System Issues

- Radiators that don't heat fully due to air locks — requires bleeding
- Boiler pressure that drops within days of commissioning — indicates a leak or system fault
- Thermostatic controls that don't respond correctly to zone settings
- Hot water that takes excessively long to arrive at taps (particularly in large homes)
- Noisy pipes — water hammer or loose clips

### 8. Roof and Loft Issues

- Ridge tiles that are inadequately pointed
- Slipped or cracked tiles on the main roof slopes
- Loft insulation that has been laid but doesn't cover the full floor area
- Soffit vents blocked by insulation — causes condensation in the loft
- Water staining on roof timbers indicating previous or current ingress

### 9. External Brickwork

- Mortar joints that are soft, recessed, or missing (known as "raked" joints) — these allow water ingress
- Cracking at the corners of window openings — can indicate lintel issues
- Efflorescence (white salt staining) — indicates moisture has passed through the brickwork

### 10. Snagged Carpets and Floor Finishes

If carpets or other floor coverings are included:
- Seams should be invisible and lying flat
- Tack strips should not be visible at doorways
- Laminate or engineered flooring should be flat with no gaps or cupped boards

## How to Get Your Builder to Fix These Defects

1. **Document everything before you contact them verbally.** Once you've called, builders sometimes argue the defect didn't exist at handover. Photos with timestamps are your evidence.
2. **Submit in writing.** Use email. A numbered list with photographs attached. Reference the relevant NHBC Buildmark category.
3. **Be specific and clear.** "Kitchen — upper cabinet to left of cooker, door 3mm lower on left hinge than right hinge, won't close flush."
4. **Set a deadline.** Give a reasonable timeframe — 30 days for non-urgent items, 7 days for leaks or electrical faults.
5. **Follow up in writing if ignored.** Never let more than 14 days pass without a written follow-up.
