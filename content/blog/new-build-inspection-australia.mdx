Taking handover of a new build home in Australia is one of the most significant financial moments of your life. It's also one of the most time-pressured — builders want the handover to happen quickly, and buyers often feel overwhelmed and unprepared to ask the right questions.

This guide gives you everything you need to conduct a thorough pre-settlement inspection, understand your rights under the Home Building Compensation (HBC) scheme, and create a defect report that your builder is legally required to address.

## Your Right to a Pre-Handover Inspection

In most Australian states, you have the right to conduct a pre-settlement inspection of your new home before signing the certificate of practical completion. The builder must give you reasonable access to the property for this purpose.

The inspection window varies by state — typically 1–5 business days before the settlement date. Don't waive this right. Even if the builder says "it's all finished," conducting a thorough independent inspection is the only way to protect yourself.

If you're not comfortable doing it alone, you can hire a registered building inspector. However, with the right checklist, most buyers can identify the vast majority of defects themselves — and document them in a format the builder must respond to.

## Understanding the HBC Scheme

The Home Building Compensation (HBC) scheme (formerly Home Warranty Insurance) protects homeowners in most Australian states against losses from defective or incomplete residential building work. Coverage varies by state:

- **NSW:** HBC covers defects for 6 years (structural) and 2 years (non-structural) from the completion date
- **VIC:** Domestic Building Insurance covers 10 years structural, 2 years non-structural
- **QLD:** Queensland Home Warranty Scheme covers defects for up to 6 years 6 months
- **SA, WA, ACT:** Similar schemes with varying time limits — check your state's consumer protection authority

Regardless of the scheme in your state, the underlying principle is the same: **defects must be documented and reported to the builder before claiming**. The HBC scheme is a backstop — it covers you if your builder can't or won't fix defects. But your first step is always to report to the builder.

## What to Check at Your Handover Inspection

### External Areas

1. **Roof:** Check from ground level that all tiles or metal roofing are correctly fixed and free of damage. Inspect any flashings at wall junctions and around penetrations.
2. **Fascias, gutters, and downpipes:** Should be correctly aligned and free of construction debris. Gutters should flow toward downpipes without sagging.
3. **External walls:** Check rendered or cladded surfaces for cracks, bubbling, or hollow areas. For brick homes, inspect mortar joints for consistency.
4. **Windows and external doors:** Test every external opening — all should open, close, and lock correctly. Check flyscreen mesh is undamaged and frames are clean.
5. **Garage door:** Test the electric operation and verify the auto-reverse safety function works. Drop a piece of paper under the door as it closes — it should reverse.
6. **Driveway and paths:** No cracks or settlement. Drainage slope should be away from the house and garage.
7. **Garden and fencing:** Turf or landscaping should be in the agreed condition. Fences should be plumb and securely posted.

### Internal — General

1. Test every power point with a socket tester. All should indicate correct polarity.
2. Test every light switch — confirm it operates the correct fitting.
3. Check the consumer (distribution) board — all circuit breakers should be labelled correctly.
4. Inspect all walls and ceilings for paint finish consistency, cracks, and paint misses. Natural light reveals far more than artificial lighting.
5. Check all internal doors — they should swing freely, latch on first swing, and close completely.
6. Skirting boards and architraves should be securely fixed with tight joints at corners.

### Kitchen

1. Open and close every cabinet and drawer — alignment and smooth operation.
2. Check benchtops for scratches, burns, and tight mitre joints.
3. Inspect splashback tiling for consistent grouting and full silicon sealing at benchtop edge.
4. Run the kitchen tap — check hot water arrives and the water pressure is adequate.
5. Check under the sink immediately after running water for any drips.
6. Test the rangehood on all settings.
7. Check all integrated appliances are correctly installed and commissioned.

### Bathrooms

1. Inspect all tiling — no lippage, consistent grout, and fully sealed silicon joints at floor/wall junctions.
2. Flush every toilet and check cistern refill time.
3. Run every shower and check drainage speed and pressure.
4. Test every tap — hot and cold.
5. Check exhaust fans operate and continue after the light switch is turned off.
6. Inspect shower screens for scratches and correct seal operation.

### Bedrooms

1. Test built-in robes — sliding doors should glide smoothly, hinged doors should hang plumb.
2. Check all power point positions match the specification.
3. Radiators or air conditioning units should be correctly installed and operational.

### Ceiling and Insulation

1. Check ceiling fixtures are flush and aligned.
2. In the roof space (if accessible), verify insulation is fully laid and free of gaps.
3. No signs of moisture or water staining on ceiling linings.

### Heating, Ventilation and Air Conditioning

1. Test ducted air conditioning in both heat and cool modes. Every outlet should deliver airflow.
2. Check any ducting visible in roof space for secure connections.
3. Test ceiling fans on all speeds if fitted.
4. Verify hot water system is set to the correct temperature (60°C minimum at cylinder for legionella prevention).

## 5 Essential Tips for Your Australian Handover Inspection

1. **Do it in daylight.** Schedule your inspection for a bright morning. Artificial lighting hides a multitude of sins.
2. **Bring a friend.** Two pairs of eyes in different rooms simultaneously means you cover more ground.
3. **Take photos of everything.** Not just defects — document the entire property condition at handover so the builder can't later claim damage was caused by you.
4. **Don't feel rushed.** You are entitled to take the time necessary for a thorough inspection. Politely decline if your builder's representative tries to hurry you.
5. **Write it down the same day.** Memories fade and photos lose context. Record every defect with room, location, and description on the day of inspection.

## After the Inspection: Getting Defects Fixed

1. Submit your defect list in writing to the builder within 7 days of handover.
2. Include photographs and specific locations for every item.
3. Request written confirmation of receipt and a schedule for repairs.
4. Under most state warranties, builders must respond within a reasonable time — typically 10–30 business days.
5. If the builder is unresponsive, contact your state's building authority:
   - NSW: Fair Trading NSW
   - VIC: Victorian Building Authority
   - QLD: QBCC (Queensland Building and Construction Commission)
   - WA: Building Commission WA
