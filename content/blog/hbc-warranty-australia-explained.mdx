Australia's Home Building Compensation (HBC) scheme is one of the most important protections available to new home buyers — but it only works if you know how to use it. Many buyers discover the scheme exists only when they have a problem, at which point they're often too late to make a claim or haven't preserved the documentation they need.

This guide explains the HBC scheme clearly: what it covers, how it varies by state, what the time limits are, and exactly what you need to do to protect your entitlements.

## What Is HBC?

Home Building Compensation (HBC) — formerly called Home Warranty Insurance (HWI) — is a mandatory insurance scheme that covers homeowners against loss caused by defective or incomplete residential building work in most Australian states and territories.

The scheme is compulsory for licensed builders undertaking residential work above certain value thresholds. When your builder takes out HBC cover on your home, they are registered with the relevant state authority and a certificate of insurance is issued. You should receive this certificate before or at settlement.

**State-by-state overview:**

| State | Scheme | Structural | Non-Structural |
|-------|--------|-----------|---------------|
| NSW | HBC (icare) | 6 years | 2 years |
| VIC | Domestic Building Insurance | 10 years | 2 years |
| QLD | QBCC Home Warranty | 6 years 6 months | 1 year |
| SA | Building Indemnity Insurance | 5 years | N/A |
| WA | Home Indemnity Insurance | 6 years | 6 years |
| ACT | Builder Warranty Insurance | 5 years | 2 years |

*Note: Coverage and limits vary. Always check the specific policy terms for your state.*

## What HBC Covers

### Defective Workmanship and Materials

HBC covers physical damage to your home caused by defective work or materials used in construction. This includes:

- Foundation and structural defects
- Roof framing defects
- External waterproofing failures
- Defective plumbing that causes damage
- Defective electrical work
- Poor finishes that cause consequential damage (for example, defective tiling that causes water ingress and subfloor damage)

The scheme distinguishes between **structural defects** (covered for the longer period) and **non-structural defects** (shorter coverage period). The definition of "structural" varies slightly by state but generally refers to defects that affect the structural integrity of the building — foundations, load-bearing walls, roof structure, and the like.

### Failure to Complete

If your builder cannot complete the work due to insolvency, death, or disappearance, HBC may cover the cost of completing your home up to the policy limit.

### Builder Insolvency or Disappearance

If your builder becomes insolvent or cannot be located after leaving defects unrepaired, HBC provides a financial safety net to arrange repairs.

## What HBC Does NOT Cover

Understanding the exclusions prevents nasty surprises:

- **Normal wear and tear** — Fading paint, carpet wear, tap washers, and other components that deteriorate through use.
- **Owner-caused damage** — Any modifications or damage caused by you, your contractors, or your actions.
- **Defects not reported within the coverage period** — If you don't report a defect before the relevant time limit expires, you lose your entitlement.
- **Consequential loss** — Lost rent, temporary accommodation costs, and other indirect losses are generally not covered.
- **Work done without a building permit** — If your builder didn't obtain required approvals, coverage may be affected.
- **Minor defects below the excess** — Some schemes have minimum claim amounts.
- **Landscaping, pools, and outbuildings** — Unless these were included in the primary building contract.

## The Most Important Rule: Report in Writing Within the Coverage Period

The most common reason HBC claims are rejected or reduced is **failure to report defects in writing before the coverage period expires**.

Under every state scheme, you must:
1. Report the defect to your builder in writing
2. Allow a reasonable period for repair
3. If unrepaired, lodge a claim with the relevant authority before the coverage period expires

**Verbal complaints, even to your builder's site manager, don't count.** You need a written record — email with date and timestamp — that you can produce as evidence.

If a defect is found two weeks before a coverage period expires, you still need to report it in writing before that date to preserve your claim.

## Time Limits: Why You Must Act

Coverage periods sound generous — 6 or 10 years — but time passes quickly. The most common defects that appear after initial settlement include:

- **Settlement cracks** — Most appear in months 3–18 as the building dries and moves
- **Water ingress** — Often only noticed after heavy rainfall, which may be months after handover
- **Tile delamination** — Can take 1–2 years to become apparent
- **Hot water system faults** — May not fail until the system is under sustained load in winter

The best protection is to conduct scheduled inspections:
- At handover (and submit initial snag list)
- At 6 months
- At 12 months
- At 18–24 months (before the non-structural period expires in most states)

Each inspection should produce a written report submitted to the builder.

## How to Make an HBC Claim

### Step 1: Document the Defect

Photograph the defect clearly. Record when you first noticed it and any evidence of when it may have started (water staining patterns, crack progression, etc.).

### Step 2: Report to Your Builder in Writing

Send a written defect notice to your builder. Include:
- Your name and address
- Description of the defect with photographs
- Location within the property
- When you first noticed it
- Your request for repair within a specified timeframe (30 days is standard)

Keep a copy of everything.

### Step 3: Allow Time for Repair

Under the HBC scheme, builders are typically given a reasonable opportunity to repair before you can claim. In most states, 30–90 days is considered reasonable, depending on the nature of the defect.

### Step 4: If Unrepaired, Contact Your State Authority

**NSW:** icare HBC — hbc.icare.nsw.gov.au
**VIC:** Victorian Building Authority — vba.vic.gov.au
**QLD:** QBCC — qbcc.qld.gov.au
**SA:** CBS — cbs.sa.gov.au
**WA:** Building Commission — buildingcommission.wa.gov.au

Each authority has an online claims portal and a dedicated team for new home warranty claims.
